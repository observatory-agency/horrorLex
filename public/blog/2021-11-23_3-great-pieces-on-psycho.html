3 Great Pieces On: Psycho

One of the first questions Professor Lex often gets is: “What’s the most written-about movie at Horror Lex?” The answer, hands down: Psycho (1960).

The sheer number of books and articles on Alfred Hitchcock’s masterpiece can be overwhelming. So, to get you started, here are 3 Great Pieces on Psycho:

  1. Linda Williams – “Learning to Scream” (Sight & Sound, Dec. 1994)

    Williams asserts that Psycho, terrifying for audiences of the day, fundamentally altered film viewing habits. Audiences learned to strap in for a roller coaster ride, marked by “repeated tension and release, assault and escape,” rather than passively receive a classical narrative.

  2. James Griffith – “Psycho: Not Guilty as Charged” (Film Comment, July/Aug. 1996)

    An intriguing piece arguing that Psycho does not implicate the audience in voyeurism, as is commonly said. Griffith also pushes back on the notion that Psycho is misogynist, pointing out its fairly egalitarian treatment of male and female characters (with an admitted asterisk on that shower scene). Griffith manages to do all this, plus a deep textual read of the film, in four pages.

  3. Laurence Rickels – The Psycho Records (Wallflower Press, 2016)

    Using a unique mix of Freudian theory, philosophy, anthropology, history, and German film expertise, as well as a healthy knowledge of pop culture both high and low, Rickels presents an elliptical stream of thought hinging on his conception of the national trauma induced by Janet Leigh’s screen death in the shower.

Paul York
